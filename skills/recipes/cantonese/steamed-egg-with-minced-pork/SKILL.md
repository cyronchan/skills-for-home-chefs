# Steamed Egg with Minced Pork 肉餅蒸水蛋

*A beloved Hong Kong home dish. Simple, cheap, deeply satisfying.*

---

## About this dish

融化肉餅蒸水蛋 is one of the most iconic Cantonese home-cooked dishes. The steamed egg is silky and just set, almost like a soft savoury custard, with seasoned minced pork running through it. It is the kind of dish that requires almost no skill but rewards patience and good technique. Eaten over white rice, it is complete comfort food.

## Ingredients

**Main ingredients:**
- 3 large eggs — essential
- 150g minced pork — essential (or minced chicken)
- 250ml warm water or warm unsalted stock — essential

**For the pork marinade:**
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- 1 tsp oyster sauce
- ½ tsp sesame oil
- ¼ tsp white pepper
- 1 tsp cornstarch

**To finish:**
- 1 tsp soy sauce (drizzled over after steaming)
- ½ tsp sesame oil
- 1 spring onion, finely sliced
- Optional: a few drops of light soy sauce and a small knob of ginger, grated

**Substitutions:**
- Minced pork → minced chicken, fish paste, or firm tofu crumbled (for vegetarian)
- Oyster sauce → vegetarian oyster sauce or hoisin
- Warm water → warm chicken or pork stock for more depth

---

## Method

1. **Marinate the pork.** Mix the minced pork with soy sauce, oyster sauce, sesame oil, white pepper, and cornstarch. Set aside for 5 minutes.

2. **Make the egg mixture.** Crack 3 eggs into a bowl and beat gently. Add 250ml of warm (not hot) water. The ratio is roughly 1 egg to 80–85ml water — this produces a silky, just-set custard. Mix gently; avoid creating bubbles.

3. **Strain the egg mixture** through a fine sieve into a shallow heatproof dish (a ceramic or glass bowl works well). This removes any stringy egg white and ensures a smooth surface.

4. **Add the pork.** Spoon the marinated minced pork over the surface of the egg mixture in an even layer. Do not stir — the pork will sink gently and distribute during steaming.

5. **Steam.** Place the dish in a steamer over medium heat. Cover the dish loosely with a plate or cling film with a few holes poked in it — this prevents condensation dripping onto the surface and creating craters. Steam for 12–15 minutes.

6. **Check doneness.** Gently shake the dish. The egg should be just set — a slight wobble in the centre is fine and will firm as it rests. If it is still completely liquid in the centre, steam for 2–3 more minutes.

7. **Finish and serve.** Remove from the steamer. Drizzle with soy sauce and sesame oil. Scatter spring onion over the top. Serve immediately over steamed white rice.

---

## Taste like a chef

- **Does it need salt?** Taste a small spoonful before adding the final soy sauce drizzle. It should be lightly savoury. The soy and sesame finish add the final seasoning.
- **Is the texture right?** It should be silky and smooth, not rubbery. Rubbery egg means it was oversteamed or the water was too hot.
- **Does it need freshness?** The spring onion is important — do not skip it. A small amount of fresh ginger grated over the top adds a clean lift.

---

## Make it yours

- **Seafood version:** Replace the pork with small prawns or crab meat for a more delicate version.
- **Richer version:** Use warm chicken stock instead of water for a deeper savouriness.
- **Simpler version:** Omit the pork entirely for a pure steamed egg (蒸水蛋) — equally delicious as a side dish.

---

## The next day

Store covered in the fridge for up to 1 day. Reheat gently by steaming for 3–4 minutes — do not microwave as it will turn rubbery. Leftover steamed egg can be broken up and stirred through congee or fried rice for an extra protein boost.

---

## Food safety note

Ensure the egg is fully set before serving. The internal temperature should reach 71°C / 160°F. Do not serve undercooked egg to young children, the elderly, pregnant people, or immunocompromised individuals.

---

## Contributor notes

The two most common mistakes: using cold water (which makes the egg tough and uneven) and steaming on high heat (which creates a pockmarked surface). Use warm water and medium heat — patience is the whole technique.
